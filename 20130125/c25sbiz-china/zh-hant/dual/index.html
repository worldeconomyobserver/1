美國小企業在中國的生意經

New Path for Trade: Selling in China

JOHN GROSSMANN
2013年1月25日
Like many American businesses fighting to keep their prices competitive, Vision Quest Lighting turned to China about six years ago. It now imports about a sixth of the two dozen to three dozen parts required to make its lighting fixtures from there. Recently, however, the Long Island company began to see China in a different light: as a sales target. The growing economy of the world’s most populous nation made it ripe for Vision Quest’s architectural lighting fixtures, many custom-made for hotel and restaurant chains like Hilton and KFC.
就像許多努力保持價格優勢的美國公司一樣，照明裝置公司Vision Quest Lighting（簡稱VQL）大約在六年前開始轉向中國。該公司製作照明裝置所需的零部件約有二三十種，其中約六分之一依賴進口。不過最近，這家位於長島的公司開始以不同的眼光看待中國：把中國當做銷售目標市場。隨着這個全球人口最多的國家經濟不斷增長，VQL公司在中國銷售建築照明裝置的時機已經成熟，該公司的許多產品都是為希爾頓和肯德基等酒店或連鎖餐飲店定製的。
When one such client, a clothing retailer, ordered 1,500 lights for five stores, Vision Quest’s chief executive, Larry Lieberman, decided it made sense to start manufacturing lights in China. Other American clients, he reasoned, would no doubt begin placing similar orders as their chains sought to capitalize on the world’s fastest-growing consumer market. And with high-quality products from the West coveted in China, Mr. Lieberman also imagined his products on display in Chinese showrooms.
當一家服裝零售商為五家商店訂購了1500盞燈具時，VQL的首席執行官(CEO)拉里·利伯曼(Larry Lieberman)拿定主意，認為開始在中國製造燈具合情合理。他考慮，隨着美國客戶的連鎖店，尋求佔據全球增長最快的消費市場，毫無疑問，它們也會開出類似的訂單。而且由於西方國家高質量的產品在中國廣受歡迎，利伯曼設想自己的產品也能在中國擺出來展示。
And yet, selling goods in China is not easy. Mr. Lieberman made the 1,500 lights only to see them gather dust in a warehouse in Guangzhou for more than four weeks because he had not yet established a local enterprise approved to process sales.
但在中國銷售產品並非易事。由於利伯曼還沒在當地建立一家有銷售資質的公司進行銷售，他只能看着做好的1500盞燈具在廣州的倉庫里擱了四個星期，靜靜落灰。
“The customer couldn’t pick up the goods because we were still trying to set up something so they could buy them correctly and pay the right tax,” he said.
他說，“顧客無法購買我們的產品，因為我們仍在努力成立公司，這樣他們就可以通過正當途徑購買產品，繳納符合要求的稅款。”
With help from an experienced consultant, Mr. Lieberman finessed the impasse by selling through an established local company, and he remains bullish on cracking the Chinese market — as do many other small-business owners. After all, China, according to a 2012 McKinsey & Company report, From Mass to Mainstream, will be the world’s largest growth market for many years.
在一位有經驗的諮詢人士的幫助下，利伯曼通過一家當地公司出售產品，巧妙地解決了困難，他像許多小企業主一樣，對打開中國市場仍持樂觀態度。畢竟麥肯錫(McKinsey & Company)在2012年發佈的報告《從大眾到主流》(From Mass to Mainstream)中指出，中國將會成為全球最大的增長型市場，這種勢頭會持續很多年。
This small-business guide offers tips for getting started based on the experiences of entrepreneurs and small businesses that have already tried.
根據已經進軍中國的企業家和小公司積累的經驗，這篇小企業指南會向正在做準備的公司提供一些建議。
BILINGUAL IS NOT BICULTURAL Lou Hoffman is founder and chief executive of the Hoffman Agency in San Jose, Calif., a communications consulting company that generates more than 50 percent of its revenue in Asia. Mr. Hoffman planted his flag in China in 1999.
掌握兩門語言不代表了解兩種文化 盧·霍夫曼(Lou Hoffman)是霍夫曼公關公司(Hoffman Agency)的創始人兼CEO，這家位於加利福尼亞州聖何塞的傳播諮詢公司有超過50%的收入來自亞洲。霍夫曼公司在1999年進入中國市場。
“I thought I was in not just another country but another universe,” he said. “It starts with the language, but goes much deeper. We couldn’t do business on the phone or by fax. Placing our first classified ad took 14 hours. We had to do everything in person, and considering the traffic in Beijing, you could kill three hours so someone could see your face.”
“我覺得我不僅僅是在另一個國家，感覺更像是在另一個宇宙，”他說，“首先是語言，但差異其實比語言更深。我們不能用電話或傳真做生意。投放第一個分類廣告花了14個小時，我們需要親自做每一件事，考慮到北京的交通狀況，你可能要花三個小時才能跟別人見上一面。”
Instead of dispatching a lieutenant from his California headquarters to open a satellite office in Beijing, Mr. Hoffman delayed expansion for nearly a year.
霍夫曼沒有從加州總部派遣一名副手，前往北京開設分公司，而是將擴張計劃推遲了將近一年。
Instead, he hired a Chinese national and embedded her in his San Jose office for 10 months so she could learn his agency’s culture, then carry it home with her. “We wanted someone able to interview people in their native tongue and able to bridge the cultures, which she was able to do,” Mr. Hoffman said.
霍夫曼聘用了一個中國人，並讓她在聖何塞的辦公室待了10個月，以便讓她了解公司的文化，然後再將這種文化帶回中國。霍夫曼說，“我們想要的是一個能用母語與別人交流，並能溝通兩種文化的人，她能做到。”
SET UP SHOP AS A WFOE Although it is possible to scout opportunities with a so-called rep office and to do business in China by selling through distributors or by licensing products to a Chinese company, most American businesses that are serious about selling in China invest the time and money to establish themselves as a wholly foreign-owned enterprise, or what is known as a WFOE (pronounced WOOF-ee). “We do probably 100 WFOEs for every rep office,” said Dan Harris,  a lawyer with the Seattle firm Harris & Moure who writes a blog about Chinese law and business. “Legal fees for company formation, trademark and employee contracts and manuals typically run around $30,000 to $45,000.”
以外商獨資企業的身份開設商店 雖然可以通過所謂的代表處來尋找機會，在中國做生意也可以通過分銷商進行銷售，或者授權中國公司代理產品，但多數認真對待中國市場的美國公司，都會投入時間和資金成立自己的外商獨資企業，英文簡稱為WFOE（讀作 WOOF-ee）。“我們大概要幫助建立100家外商獨資企業，才會幫助建立一家代表處，”西雅圖Harris & Moure律師事務所律師丹·哈里斯(Dan Harris)說。“公司組建、註冊商標，撰寫勞動合同和各種指南，所需的法律費用約為3萬美元（約合18.6萬元人民幣）至4.5萬美元。
But the upfront investment does not stop there. Depending on the location and the type of business, the Chinese government has minimum capital requirements — money deposited in a Chinese bank account — that can range “from $15,000 to millions of dollars,” he said
但前期投資還不止這些。中國政府會根據位置和企業類型設定最低資本金要求，這些錢需要存入一家中資銀行的賬戶中。他表示，最低資本要求“從1.5萬美元到數百萬美元”不等。
And think months, not weeks, to get all of the paperwork approved. “In China, you can’t do anything last minute,” said Savio S. Chan, president and chief executive of U.S. China Partners, which is based in Great Neck, N.Y., and which helped Vision Quest move its light fixtures out of regulatory limbo. “It can easily take up to six months to set up a WFOE.”
要讓所有文件通過審批，你得準備幾個月的時間，而不僅要幾周。“在中國，任何事都不能留到最後一刻再做，”總部位於紐約大頸(Great Neck)的美中夥伴公司(US China Partners)總裁兼CEO陳少宏(Savio S. Chan)說。“經常要花半年時間才能成立起一家外商獨資企業。”正是該公司幫助VQL的燈具業務走出了監管方面的困境。
LET OTHERS OPEN THE DOOR Cabot Hosiery Mills, which makes high-end recreational socks in Northfield, Vt., has edged into the Chinese market. Sought out by a Chinese distributor at an American trade show, the company has traded a bit of profit potential to test the demand for its made-in-America goods without wrangling a WFOE or staffing a sales operation.
讓其他人為你開門 生產高端休閑短襪的Cabot Hosiery Mills公司位於佛蒙特州諾斯菲爾德，該公司已經進入了中國市場。在美國的一次貿易展上，一家中國經銷商找到了該公司。該公司不用費力設立一家外商獨資企業，也不用僱人建立銷售團隊，就能檢驗該公司美國製造的產品在中國市場的反響。作為代價它，只會損失了一點潛在利潤。
“Right now, it’s very straightforward and still small, less than 1 percent of our volume,” said Ric Cabot, chief executive of the company, which owns the Darn Tough Vermont brand. “But if it gets to the point where we see we’re leaving too much money on the table, we might consider doing something different.”
“目前，這種模式非常簡單，業務量依然很小，在我們的總銷售額中所佔比例還不到1%，”公司首席執行官里克·卡伯特(Ric Cabot)說。Darn Tough Vermont品牌即為該公司所有。“但如果我們發現自己把錢都留給別人賺了，我們可能就會考慮採取不同的行動。”
DON’T GET KNOCKED OFF Product infringement and knockoffs are risks in China. As a result, Earl Kluft, owner of E.S. Kluft & Company in Rancho Cucamonga, Calif., a maker of luxury mattresses priced from $3,500 to $70,000, watched his first attempt to tap into the Chinese hunger for premium Western brands fall apart.
不要被仿冒 產品侵權和仿製是在中國做生意的風險。因此，加利福尼亞州庫卡蒙格牧場(Rancho Cucamonga)的E.S. Kluft & Company公司老闆厄爾·克魯夫特(Earl Kluft)眼睜睜看着自己第一次嘗試失敗了，他本想利用中國人對西方高檔品牌的強烈渴望來賺錢。他的公司生產豪華床墊，價格在3500美元至7萬美元之間。
“A huge manufacturer of recliners and small mattresses came to us, and we started a program under their name,” Mr. Kluft said, explaining that the arrangement started with six mattress products named for American cities. But on successive trips to China, he started to see fewer of his products on display — and more of other brands that looked very much like his.
“一家生產躺椅和小床墊的大型生產商找到我們，我們就以他們的名義開始了一個項目，”克魯夫特說。他解釋說，根據項目安排，先生產六款床墊產品，分別以美國的城市命名。但連續多次前往中國後，他開始注意到，展出的床墊中，他公司的產品越來越少，而其他品牌的產品卻越來越多，這些產品看上去與他的產品極為相似。
“Even after we stopped selling to them, they still had my picture up,” he said. Networking through a friend, Mr. Kluft has since signed a deal with a Chinese division of an Indonesian company that cautiously re-established a sales channel with minimal upfront investment. “The idea is to get this up and running,” he said. “We charge them a royalty, so much a year for use of our name in their stores, and they buy the product at a special discount.”
“即使在我們停止向它們出售產品後，他們依然掛着我的照片，”他說。通過一位朋友，克魯夫特開始認識更多人，自此與一家印度尼西亞的工作公司簽下了一單交易，以最少的前期投資，謹慎地再建立起一座銷售渠道。“我的想法是，希望業務開始運作起來，”他說。“我們向他們收取專利使用費，商定他們每年在店裡使用我們的名字要付的金額，他們也以特價購買我們的產品。”
Mr. Harris, the lawyer, advises getting started by finding reliable partners on the ground. “Find them through people you know, and then pay for whatever due diligence is necessary to make sure that you have made the right choice,” he said. “And do all of this beforeyou start doing business with them.”
哈里斯律師建議首先要在當地找到信得過的合作夥伴。“通過你認識的人找到他們，然後，出錢進行任何所需的盡職調查，確保你的選擇正確，”他說，“這一切都要在你開始和他們做生意之前進行。”
LOOK LOCALLY BEFORE LEAPING For some entrepreneurs, help may be surprisingly close at hand. Many states — including Georgia, Pennsylvania, Mississippi and Tennessee — have international trade programs that offer counsel.
採取行動之前先在本地打望 對一些企業家來說，援助可能近在咫尺。包括喬治亞州、賓夕法尼亞州、密西西比州和田納西州在內的許多州，都有提供諮詢服務的國際貿易項目。
“We think it’s hard for a small company who’s never been to China to figure this out all by themselves,” said Samar Ali, assistant commissioner of international affairs in Tennessee. “We’ll help them see if there’s a need for their product in China and to think it through: Do they need to set up a WFOE? Do they need to have a presence or not? Should they go the e-commerce route? And tell them how much they should budget going forward.”
“我們覺得，對從未去過中國的小企業而言，它們很難完全靠自己弄明白這些，”田納西州負責國際事務的助理專員薩馬爾·阿里(Samar Ali)說，“我們會幫助他們了解中國對它們的產品有沒有需求，還要從各環節考慮：它們需要成立外商獨資企業嗎？它們是否需要在當地成立公司？它們是不是應該採取電子商務的方式？我們還會告訴它們，未來應準備多少預算。”
The assistance includes the use of Tennessee’s China offices for meetings with potential partners and help with business-to-business matchmaking through companies the international trade program has vetted in the 10 largest Chinese cities.
他們提供的幫助包括，使用田納西州在中國的辦公室同潛在合作夥伴開會，通過國際貿易項目在中國十大城市審核過的企業，協助他們進行一對一的配對。
Though bullish on the opportunities, Ms. Ali finds herself repeating mantras like: “Don’t go in too fast. Don’t go in blind. And don’t leave your common sense at home.”
儘管很看好這樣的機遇，但阿里發現自己在重複她說過多次的話：“不要操之過急。不要盲目行事。別忘記你所了解的基本常識。”
