中国P2P平台接连倒闭，投资者赴北京抗议遭打压

As Chinese Investors Panic Over Dubious Products, Authorities Quash Protests

曹莉, 艾莎, 黄瑞黎
2018年8月10日
In online chat groups, thousands of small investors who had put their savings into dubious digital products plotted a big protest in Beijing this week.
在网上的聊天群里，数以千计把积蓄投入可疑的网络投资产品的小投资者，策划了本周在北京举行的一场大规模抗议活动。
Under pressure from local officials, one woman’s family begged her not to go. After a visit from the local police, a man’s boss threatened to break his contract if he participated. At airports, bus and train stations across China, scores of people making their way to Beijing were confronted by police officers who urged them — sometimes with force — to turn around.
在地方官员施压之下，一名女子的家人请求她不要前往。在当地警方找上门后，一名男子的老板威胁他，一旦参与，就把他开除。在全国各地的机场、汽车站和火车站，许多前往北京的人遭到警方拦截，要求他们掉头回去，有时候还动用了武力。
Those who did make it to the headquarters of the Chinese banking regulator in Beijing on Monday faced hundreds of police officers sent to quash it. Some were dragged off, while others were shoved into buses leaving the city.
那些最终在周一到达北京、来到中国的银行监管机构总部的人，则面临着数以百计被派至现场平息抗议的警察。一些人被强行拖走，还有人被推上大巴带离北京。
For Chinese officials, the financial is quickly becoming political.
对中国的官员来说，金融问题迅速政治化。
广告
In recent years, millions of ordinary investors across China have plowed their savings into online financial products that promised big profits with little risk. But the sudden collapse of hundreds of these peer-to-peer lenders in recent months has prompted a panic, leaving many investors with little information and little way to get their money back.
近年来，中国各地数以百万计的普通投资者将积蓄投入互联网金融产品，它们承诺高回报、低风险。但近几个月里，数以百计这样的P2P借贷公司突然倒闭，引发恐慌，许多投资者拥有极少的信息，而且也没有把钱要回来的办法。
Frustrated investors are appealing to local and national authorities. As many see it, the government encouraged the growth of these online lenders, which were intended to connect mom-and-pop investors with borrowers. The state, these investors say, should make them whole.
不满的投资者向当地及国家机关求助。在许多人看来，政府鼓励了这些网上借贷公司的发展，原本这些借贷公司是为了将家庭投资者与借款人联系起来的。这些投资者说，国家应该补偿他们的损失。
While the Communist Party always tries to tamp down protests to maintain social stability, the current discontent among investors belies a bigger problem for the Chinese authorities. A vast, unruly shadow banking system, where some companies have lured people with get-rich-quick schemes, has created hidden debt bombs that could leave the economy vulnerable. And it comes at a tricky time for the leadership, as China deals with signs of slowing growth, a wavering stock market and an escalating trade war with the United States.
尽管共产党为了维持社会稳定，常常打压抗议活动，但投资者目前的不满情绪却掩盖了中国当局所面临的一个更大的问题：一个庞大、不受束缚的影子银行系统。在其中，一些公司以暴富计划引诱人们，制造出隐藏的债务炸弹，让经济变得脆弱。此外，随着中国应对经济增长放缓的迹象、股市震荡及中美贸易战升级，对中国领导层来说，这件事来得不是时候。
“I don’t believe in the government,” said Liu Yu, a 35-year-old shopkeeper who traveled 13 hours to Beijing on Monday to protest. He said he lost $9,000 on a lending platform called Catch the Money Cat, which notified investors on July 20 that it was having problems paying back investors and that the police in the city of Hangzhou were investigating. “There is no credibility,” he added.
“我觉得现在我不相信政府，”35岁的刘玉（音）说。开商店的他经过了13个小时的旅程于周一抵达北京，参加抗议活动。他说，自己在一个名为“抓钱猫”的借贷平台上损失了9000美元，7月20日，该平台通知投资者，它在还款方面存在困难，而杭州警方正在展开调查。“没有公信力，”他补充道。
When these digital products emerged in China a decade ago, they were pitched as an outlet for small-time borrowers, who were mostly ignored by the country’s big state-run banks. For investors ponying up the money to lend, the gains were tantalizing, with some promoting returns as high as 40 or 50 percent.
10年前，当这些网络投资产品出现在中国时，它们被定位为向小规模借贷者提供贷款的渠道，这些借款人大多被大型国有银行所忽视。对于出钱放贷的投资者来说，收益十分诱人，一些产品宣传回报率高达40%或50%。
Lightly regulated, they attracted ordinary people — from students to grandparents — in droves through an advertising blitz on mobile apps, in movie theaters and even in residential elevators. Many products appeared to have the government’s imprimatur, since they used state-owned banks to help facilitate payments and create the appearance of safer investments. Thousands of companies pulled in an estimated $194 billion from as many as four million individuals.
由于监管不力，它们通过在移动应用程序、电影院，甚至是居民楼电梯里大打广告战，吸引了从学生到老年人的大批普通人。许多产品似乎获得了政府的认可，因为它们利用国有银行帮助推进支付，并且打造出一种安全投资的假象。据估计，有数以千计的公司从多达400万的个人那里吸收了1940亿美元的资金。
广告
Tang Zehui, 42, a factory worker who rode a 30-hour train to Beijing this week from southwestern Sichuan Province, said she had turned to these investments, as many others in China had, because the interest rate on regular savings accounts was too low.
42岁的汤泽辉（音）是一名工厂工人，本周，她坐了30个小时的火车，从西南部的四川省来到北京。她表示自己之所以像其他许多中国人一样进行这样的投资，是因为把钱放在银行的利息太低。
“We don’t have any good ways to manage our finances,” said Ms. Tang, who said she lost about $22,000 to Leadercf.com which is based in the southern city of Guangzhou. Ms. Tang said she trusted it because the state’s support seemed to be implicit.
“我们也没有什么好的理财方法，”汤泽辉说，她表示自己在礼德财富上损失了约2.2万美元，该公司总部位于南部城市广州。汤泽辉说她之所以信任这个平台，是因为该平台似乎暗示有政府支持。
The platforms were supposed to be matchmakers, providing a place for savers to meet borrowers who needed short-term loans. But with little regulation, many of these companies pooled together investors’ money and then tried to find borrowers, creating a potential mismatch when it came time to pay out.
这些平台本应起到中间人的作用，为储户提供一个场所，可以遇到需要短期贷款的借款人。但由于缺乏监管，这些公司中有许多都将投资者的资金集中起来，然后试图寻找借款人，在支付时产生潜在的不匹配。
In 2016, they were told to register with local financial regulators and comply with new rules or face being shut down. But the deadline was postponed to an unspecified date.
2016年，它们被告知要在当地金融监管机构注册并遵守新的规则，否则将面临关闭。但最后期限被推迟到一个未指明的日期。
“Many people see these products as similar to saving,” said Yu Baicheng, director of research at Wangdaizhijia, an independent provider of data on China’s online lending industry. “They are not aware of the potential risks.”
“很多人把它们当成存款，”中国在线借贷行业数据独立提供商网贷之家研究院院长于百程表示。“风险意识太欠缺。”
There have been blowups in the past. The founder of a $9 billion lending platform called Ezubao was sentenced last year to life in prison for bilking investors. In January, more than $5 billion of investor money disappeared from a popular investment portal called Qianbao, which had been praised by the official state news media.
过去也曾出过爆炸性事件。规模达90亿美元的贷款平台e租宝的创始人因诈骗，去年被判处无期徒刑。今年1月，超过50亿美元的投资资金从一个名为钱宝的热门投资门户网站中消失，该网站曾经受到官方新闻媒体的好评。
广告
A deeper worry started to set in this summer, after the government warned about the risk of losses.
在政府对亏损风险发出警告之后，今年夏天，更深层次的担忧开始出现。
“High returns mean high risks,” Guo Shuqing, the chairman of China’s Banking Insurance Regulatory Commission, said in June in public remarks at a conference. Any product that offers an 8 percent return, Mr. Guo said, is “very dangerous.” Investors in products with returns of 10 percent or more, he went on, should “be prepared to lose all the principal.”
“高收益意味着高风险，”中国银行业保险监督管理委员会主席郭树清今年6月在一次会议上的公开讲话中表示。郭树清说，任何提供8%回报的产品都“很危险”。他还说，购买回报率为10%或更高产品的投资者应该“准备损失全部本金”。
Almost immediately, the concerns prompted a run on the industry, as investors demanded their money back. A wave of defaults followed, with each collapse setting off the next, like a set of dominoes.
随着投资者要求退款，这些担忧几乎立即引发了对该行业的冲击。随之而来的是一波违约，每次违约都会引发下一轮违约，就像多米诺骨牌一样。
Some executives shut down their businesses and fled with client money, while others suspended operations following a flood of demands from investors for repayment. Many are now under investigation.
一些高管关闭了业务，带着客户资金“跑路”；另一些高管在投资者大量提出偿还要求后暂停了业务。许多人正在接受调查。
In early July, Money Pig, a company based in the southern city of Shenzhen, notified tens of thousands of investors that their accounts had been suspended. Trying to get their money back, investors gathered at a police station there a few weeks later. The group of office workers, students and businessmen wore face masks, sunglasses and white T-shirts that read, “Give me my hard-earned money back!” and “Government, save our investors!”
7月初，位于南方城市深圳的发财猪公司通知成千上万的投资者，他们的账户已被暂停。几周后，想收回钱的投资者聚集在当地的公安部门。这些办公室职员、学生和商人戴着口罩和墨镜，身穿白色T恤，上面写着“还我血汗钱！”和“政府救救投资人！”。
“I started to panic,” said Chen Shuaipeng, 30, a salesman who flew to Shenzhen for the protest from the eastern city of Tianjin. He said he had $93,000 of savings in a Money Pig account before it was frozen.
“我真的开始慌了，”30岁的陈帅鹏（音）说，他是一名推销员，从东部城市天津飞到深圳参加抗议活动。他说，在发财猪的帐户被冻结之前，他存了9.3万美元。
In Hangzhou, a group of investors who showed up to talk to officials was large enough to fill two sports stadiums, according to state news media reports. Others have gathered to protest in Nanjing and Shanghai, according to news reports and widely circulated videos online.
据官方媒体报道，在杭州，前来与官员会谈的投资者足以塞满两座体育馆。根据新闻报道和广泛流传的网络视频，南京和上海也有很多人聚集起来抗议。
“The entire industry is facing panic, and the speed of collapse is still accelerating,” said Xue Hongyan, director of Suning Financial Research Institute, which studies the industry.
“整个行业都面临恐慌的情绪，现在速度还在加快，”研究该行业的苏宁金融研究所所长薛洪言说。
Officials trying to control the situation are faced with a fast-moving crisis. In July alone, 168 lending platforms halted their operations, according to Wangdaizhijia.
试图控制局势的官员面临着一场迅速发展的危机。根据网贷之家的说法，仅在7月份就有168个贷款平台停止运营。
广告
In recent weeks, large numbers of disgruntled investors have started to share their experiences online, updating one another about investigations. Soon, investors were talking about banding together for a big protest in Beijing on Monday.
最近几周，大量心怀不满的投资者开始在网上分享经验，交流调查近况。很快，投资者们就开始谈论周一在北京举行一次大规模抗议活动。
China’s censors quickly took note, and soon one chat room after another was closed down. Investors switched between different chat apps to avoid censorship, using forums like QQ, WeChat, YeeCall, Qunduoduo and Telegram.
中国的审查机构很快就注意到了这件事，没过多久，一个又一个聊天群被关闭了。为了避免审查，投资者在不同的聊天应用之间切换，使用QQ、微信、一块、群多多和Telegram等工具交流。
Mr. Gao, who asked not to use his full name for fear that he might lose his job at a machine manufacturing company, first learned online about plans for the protest in Beijing. He had lost nearly $126,000 when the lending portal Tangxiaoseng collapsed.
高先生先是在网上了解到去北京抗议的计划。当借贷门户网站唐小僧倒塌时，他损失了近12.6万美元。由于担心失去在一家机器制造公司的工作，他不愿透露全名。
After Mr. Gao discussed his trip to Beijing in several WeChat groups, the local police went to his home. “I said one word in our chat group, and they came to my door, asking me not to go petitioning.”
高先生在几个微信群里中讨论了他的北京之行后，当地警方来到了他的家。“我群里说一句话，警察就来敲我的门，让我不要去上访。”
But Mr. Gao said he felt emboldened to let the government know what had happened to him. Quoting China’s premier, Li Keqiang, Mr. Gao said, “if you can’t see sunshine where you are, please come to Beijing.”
但是高先生表示，他有勇气让政府知道他的遭遇。他引用中国国家总理李克强的话说，“假如你在地方见不到阳光，请你到北京来。”
He went to Beijing a few days before the planned protests only to discover that a dozen government officials had visited his company and urged his boss to ask him to return. When he got home on Monday, his company asked him to sign a letter promising not to engage in protests; otherwise he would be fired.
他在计划的抗议活动前几天去了北京，结果发现有十几名政府官员到他的公司，要求他的老板让他回来。当他周一回到家时，公司要求他签署一份不参加抗议的保证书；否则他会被解雇。
广告
“I think I won’t be able to keep my job,” he said.
“我感觉我的工作保不住了，”他说。
